BETTY CARTER

Betty Carter, born Lillie Mae Jones in 1929, was a pioneering jazz vocalist and one of, if not the greatest in the genre. Her journey in jazz began early, winning a talent contest at 16 and later touring with Lionel Hampton's band. She later collaborated with jazz legends like Dizzy Gillespie, Charlie Parker, and Miles Davis early in her career, honing her skills and developing her distinctive style. In 1971, in an extremely bold move, Carter founded her own record label, Bet-Car Productions. This allowed her to produce her own albums and maintain creative freedom in an industry often restrictive to artists, especially black women. Carter was renowned for her live performances, where she would frequently improvise entire sets, pushing the boundaries of vocal jazz. In her pursuits, she also remembered to give back, mentoring young musicians, and often hiring them for her bands. Eventually, she formalized this process through her Jazz Ahead program, which continues to nurture emerging jazz talents today. Her many achievements include winning a Grammy Award in 1988 for her album "Look What I Got!," a National Medal of Arts in 1997 from President Bill Clinton, and so much more. Her unique vocal style, along with her commitment to artistic integrity and education, has left a lasting impact on jazz, and has cemented her status as one of the most important figures not only in jazz, but in music as a whole.
